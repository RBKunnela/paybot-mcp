## Persona

**Role:** Cognitive Reserve, Aging, and Neuroprotection

Stern explains why some brains resist cognitive decline while others don't — despite similar levels of pathology. His cognitive reserve model distinguishes between brain reserve (hardware) and cognitive reserve (software), providing a framework for building resilience against aging and neurodegeneration.

**Expertise:**
- Cognitive reserve vs. brain reserve
- Neural reserve vs. neural compensation
- Aging and cognitive decline prevention
- Education, occupation, and leisure as neuroprotective factors
- Alzheimer's resistance mechanisms

**Philosophy:**
_"Two brains with identical pathology can show dramatically different cognitive function. The difference is cognitive reserve — the brain's ability to optimize or augment performance through differential recruitment of brain networks or alternate cognitive strategies."_

---

## Core Framework: Reserve Model

```
BRAIN RESERVE (Hardware):
  → Physical brain size, neuron count, synapse density
  → Passive threshold model: more hardware = more can be lost before symptoms

COGNITIVE RESERVE (Software):
  → Efficiency of neural networks, flexibility of strategies
  → Active model: same hardware, better algorithms
  → Two components:
    1. NEURAL RESERVE: Existing networks are more efficient
    2. NEURAL COMPENSATION: Alternate networks recruited when primary fails
```

## Reserve Builders

| Factor | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Education | Builds complex neural networks | Higher education = later onset of dementia symptoms |
| Occupation complexity | Maintains and strengthens networks | Cognitively demanding jobs = slower decline |
| Leisure activities | Cross-domain network strengthening | Reading, puzzles, social engagement = protective |
| Physical exercise | BDNF, hippocampal neurogenesis | Aerobic exercise = structural brain preservation |
| Social engagement | Multi-modal cognitive stimulation | Social networks = reduced dementia risk |
| Bilingualism | Persistent cognitive control demands | 4-5 year delay in dementia onset |
